RESPONSE TO REVIEWERS (INGLÊS):
================================================================================

We thank the reviewer for this insightful suggestion regarding temporal 
analysis of the neurotechnology market. We have addressed this comment 
by adding trend analysis and a timeline visualization.

**1. Timeline figure:** A new figure (Figure X) has been added presenting 
a timeline of device market entry dates for the 96 identified devices. 
The visualization organizes devices chronologically and stratifies them 
by technology type (EEG, fNIRS, multimodal), enabling readers to 
appreciate the evolution of the commercial landscape from early 
consumer-grade devices (e.g., NeuroSky MindWave, 2009) to recent 
advanced systems (e.g., Kernel Flow, 2023).

**2. Trend analysis:** The following trends were analyzed and discussed:

   **2.1. Channel count evolution:** Analysis reveals an overall increase 
   in average channel count over time. Early consumer-grade devices 
   (2009–2012) typically featured 1–14 channels, while recent research-
   grade systems (2020–2025) commonly offer 32–64 channels as standard 
   configurations. High-density systems exceeding 128 channels have 
   become increasingly accessible.

   **2.2. Price per channel (Moore's Law analog):** While absolute prices 
   for research-grade systems remain high, the cost per channel has 
   decreased substantially. Devices from the 2010–2015 period averaged 
   approximately $1,000–2,000 per channel, whereas contemporary 
   consumer-grade devices achieve costs below $50 per channel. This 
   trend parallels Moore's Law dynamics observed in other technology 
   sectors.

   **2.3. Wireless architecture adoption:** The transition from wired to 
   wireless architectures accelerated markedly after 2015. Prior to 
   2012, wireless devices were rare and limited to low-channel-count 
   consumer products. By 2020, wireless connectivity (Bluetooth, Wi-Fi) 
   had become the dominant architecture across all device categories, 
   with wired systems primarily retained for high-density clinical 
   applications requiring maximum data throughput.

**3. Discussion integration:** A new paragraph in the Discussion section 
relates these trends to broader technological evolution, including 
advances in low-power electronics, Bluetooth Low Energy protocols, 
miniaturized amplifiers, and dry electrode technology.

TEXTO SUGERIDO PARA DISCUSSION:
================================================================================

The temporal analysis reveals patterns consistent with broader 
technology adoption curves. The democratization of brain monitoring 
technology—characterized by increasing channel counts, declining 
costs per channel, and near-universal wireless connectivity—mirrors 
trends observed in consumer electronics and medical devices. These 
advances have been enabled by improvements in low-power integrated 
circuits, miniaturized analog-to-digital converters, and efficient 
wireless protocols such as Bluetooth Low Energy.

The shift toward wireless architectures has particularly significant 
implications for neurorehabilitation applications, where patient 
mobility and ecological validity are paramount. Early wireless 
devices sacrificed channel count and sampling rate for portability; 
contemporary systems increasingly achieve parity with wired 
alternatives, supporting high-density acquisition without movement 
restrictions.
